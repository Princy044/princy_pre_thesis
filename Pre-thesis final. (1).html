1.0 Introduction

The increase in demand for renewable energy is one of the driving force for the development of efficient wind power generated from wind turbines. Increasing number of countries especially Europe are switching to renewable energy such as wind and solar. Renewable energy plays an important role in producing clean energy because it does not require fuel and generates no greenhouse gases. In Europe, the use of renewable energy lowers the dependency on imported fossil fuel and makes energy production sustainable. Increased interest and rise in demand for renewable energy made wind energy more attractive and calls for an efficient energy production. Modern wind turbines maximise the effective wind energy produced by operating at any wind speed. The modern wind turbine employs variable-speed-generator technologies and lot of power electronics converters as part of grid connections. On the other hand, high voltage direct current (HVDC) is the only practical solution for non-synchronous interconnected AC power system with different frequencies, for transmitting power undersea, for the long distance transmission(>100km) and large offshore wind farm (>400 MW) grid connection. In HVDC, for converting AC to DC or DC to AC there is a need for large number of power electronics devices. The drawback of using power electronics is that they produce harmonics. Harmonic regulations imposed by IEEE standards 519 should be followed strictly due to increase in use of power electronics and their effects. Therefore, the understanding of harmonics and their analysis can help to minimise the effects and increase power system stability and efficiency.

1.1 Objective of study

The primary objective is to study about harmonics in wind turbines and HVDC for onshore and offshore wind farms. To inject harmonics into the wind turbine generators in wind farm model and into converters in HVDC model present in DigSILENT PowerFactory. To simulate the model in PowerFactory and perform harmonic load flow to calculate for total harmonic distortion (THD). The secondary objective is to design harmonic filters to mitigate the harmonics produced by the power electronics in the model, such that the THD values does not exceed standard given by IEEE 519. Furthermore, to find the correct placement of the filter in the model for better performance.

2.0Literature Survey

2.1Wind turbines

Wind turbine are evolved from windmills, they are energy converters which convert kinetic wind energy to electricity. In recent past, the modern wind turbines are designed to generate optimum power at varying wind speed by introducing a lot of power electronics.

2.1.1 Types of Wind Turbines:

Wind turbine are classified into different types based on various factors.

Based on the physical appearances of the wind turbine are classified into horizontal or vertical based on rotor axis orientation and on number of blades one, two or three. In modern wind turbine horizontal axis and three blades are in use.

Classification of wind turbines on controls are pitch, stall, yaw or aerodynamics surfaces based on power control and yaw control is classified into active or free. Various generator technologies are used for wind turbines some of them are: Asynchronous generators: for variable speed wind turbine system

•DFIG- Double fed induction generator

•WRIG- Wound rotor induction generator

•SCIG- Squirrel-cage induction generator Synchronous generator: for fixed speed wind turbine system

•WRSG- Wound rotor synchronous generator

•PMSG- Permanent Magnet synchronous generator

Full converter generator used for variable speed and also for fixed speed.

2.1.2 Common type of wind generators

There are two most commonly used variable speed wind generators are:

a) Double fed induction generator (DFIG)

In DFIG, both stator and rotor windings are connected to the source. The stator of the generator is directly connected to the grid while the rotor is connected to the grid via back-to-back converter. The back-to-back converter or AC-DC-AC converter on the rotor side consists of two VSC (Voltage-sourced converter). The one connected on the rotor side is known as rotor-side converter (RSC) and the one on the grid side as grid-

3

side converter (GSC). Between the two converters a dc-link capacitor is connected, to minimise the ripples in dc-link voltage. (Refer 2.2.7 for detail on VSC)

The function of RSC is to control the speed or torque of the rotor and also power factor on the stator while the function of the GSC is to keep the dc-link voltage constant in spite of changes in magnitude and direction rotor power direction. [1]

The AC-DC-AC converter play a major role in variable speed wind turbines by converting the output of generator which is variable voltage and variable frequency into fixed voltage and fixed frequency.

Figure 1 connection of DFIG to the source and the grid.

b) Full-scale converter (FSC) generator

The full-scale converter can be used with different generator technologies and gearbox solutions to achieve high-speed, medium-speed and low-speed concepts. The generators typically used are synchronous (PMSG) and asynchronous generators. In FSC type the generator is decoupled from the grid by the converters. FSC in wind turbines allows to extract all power from wind and transfer power to grid through power electronics, which allows us to control and optimise all the power going into the grid. As a result, high efficiency of power is generated for wide speed range, low mechanical vibration and low maintenance is required. This makes FSC more opt for offshore wind turbines.

2.2 High Voltage Direct Current (HVDC)

HVDC is classified into three types based on transmission link based and 2 types based on converter type.

2.2.1Types of HVDC Transmission System

a)Monopolar HVDC link

Figure 3 Monopolar HVDC link

In a monopolar link sending and receiving end converters are connected by a single conductor. It can be operated either in negative or positive polarity, but usually preferred negative polarity due to less corona effect [2]. Here, the return path is provided by ground or water. It is cost effective, particularly for the cable transmission [3].

In place of ground return, a metallic return may be used for the situations where the earth resistivity is too high or possible interference with underwater or underground metallic structure is not suitable (subject of corrosion). [4]

b) Bipolar HVDC link

Figure 4 Bipolar HVDC link

5

A bipolar link consists of two conductors, where one is positive and another is negative. In each terminal there are two converters of equal rated voltage which are connected in series on the DC side and where the junctions between converters are grounded. In normal condition the currents in the two poles are equal and there is negligible ground current [4].

Here the two poles can operate independently so if one pole is isolated due to a fault of its conductor, the other pole can operate with the ground. In situation where the earth resistivity is high or the ground current are not tolerable, a third conductor is used as a metallic neutral. It works as a return path when there is imbalance during bipolar operation or when one pole is out of service [3].

c) Homopolar HVDC link

Figure 5 Homopolar HVDC link

The Homopolar link has the two or more conductors, all having the same polarity. Usually negative polarity is preferred due to the less corona effect and return path is grounded. When there is a fault occurred in one conductor, the whole converter is available for feeding the remaining conductor(s). Due to having some overload capability, it can carry more load than normal operation. Where the continuous ground current is acceptable, homopolar configuration offers an advantage over bipolar system, because in a bipolar system reconnection of the whole converter to a one pole of the line is more complicated and not feasible [3].

They can vary their output voltages in different level. Multilevel converters allow synthesis of higher voltage levels,

2.3 Harmonics

The ideal generated voltage or current is a sinusoidal waveform with frequency of 50Hz (0r 60Hz) with constant magnitude. Harmonics occur when the voltage and current waveform deviate from their original sine waveform. The harmonic signal frequency is a deviation from the fundamental frequency. Integer-Harmonics are integer multiple of fundamental frequency, the harmonic of order ‘n’ in a signal with a frequency that is ‘n’ times the fundamental frequency. In a balanced system, the Integer-harmonics are classified based on harmonic order into positive-sequence negative-sequence and zero-sequence.

2.3.1 Harmonic sources

The reason for both current and voltage harmonics are due to the presence of non-linear loads. The power electronics are non-linear loads that draws non-sinusoidal currents, when this current is distorted it causes voltage drop all along the power system which causes voltage distortion.

The first common type of harmonic sources is the use of a large number of small nonlinear components in the power system, e.g. televisions, personal computers, and ﬂuorescent lamps. They are mainly diode bridge rectiﬁers acting as DC current source or DC voltage source [7]

The next type of harmonic source are the largely varying non-linear loads like arc furnace, large converters connected to the grid which are used in High Voltage Direct Current (HVDC) transmission and the modern wind turbine installed in grid contains

power electronics converters which is a part of full-power converter or doubly-fed induction generator [8]

2.3.2 Effects of harmonics

Harmonic distortion cause deleterious effects such as transformer, capacitor, motor or generator overheating. The presence of harmonic current can cause thermal stress which leads to copper losses, iron losses and dielectric losses. Harmonic currents in electric machine armatures can generate pulsating electromagnetic torques Malfunctioning of electronic equipment because the normal operation happens only with pure sinusoidal voltage and current wave, inductive interference with telephone circuits. Harmonics increases the reactive power which may lead to capacitor bank failure and harmonic amplification.

It gets worse if resonant condition is present. Resonance occurs when a harmonic frequency produced by a nonlinear load closely coincides with the natural frequency of the electrical power system leading to overloading and excessive current in power system [9]

With the increase in frequency, ac current distribution becomes steeper. In cables, the current density increases from centre to the surface. The surface is less bound by the magnetic flow than the centre because of the increase in ac current the voltage is longitudinally induced. Therefore there is increase in current density in the conductor from inside to the outer layer which leads to skin effect.

2.3.3 Harmonic analysis:

Estimating the harmonics produced by the sources which are non-linear load is necessary but it is not straightforward. There is an interaction between the harmonic producing equipment, which can have varied topologies, and the electrical system.

Harmonics when not treated properly produce major power quality concern. Harmonic analysis helps to treat the problem specifically by calculating the amount of harmonics created by a specific source. Calculating the magnitude and angle of higher order harmonics of periodic waveforms is crucial for analysis, this can be done using Fourier series.

2.3.7 Harmonics solutions:

According to the literature, there are different types of harmonic solutions. It is always recommended to treat them at the source, it not only reduces effects on the consumer side but it also avoid overheating of generators.

A choke also known as smoothing reactor is used at the DC side of the converter to limit the higher order harmonics. The choke acts as the inductor, it works by smoothing any current spikes or ac without affecting DC current.

Using 12 or 18 pulse rectifier instead of 6-pulse rectifier. The rectifier draws trapezoidal current which contains the fundamental frequency sine wave and also harmonics of order depending on the number of thyristors used. [12]

For 6-pulse bridge the harmonic order is given by 6n±1 i.e., 5,7,11 … 25, for 12-pulse bridge the harmonic order 12n±1 i.e., 11, 13, 23 …. 49 and similarly, for 18-pulse

bridge it is 18n±1.

Most effective method for harmonic mitigation according to the literature is the use of harmonic filters. There are active and passive types of harmonic filter. They trap the harmonics by reducing the harmonic amplitude and it works best when connected in shunt to the source.

c) High pass filter:

High-pass filter are known by their small impedance value above the corner frequency. High pass filter draws a considerable percentage of frequency harmonic currents above the corner frequency. Therefore, this frequency must be placed below all harmonic currents that have an important presence in the installation. [13]

First-order high-pass filters are characterized by large power losses at fundamental frequency that is why they are less common. The second-order high-pass filter is the simplest to apply and provides a fairly good filtering action and reduces energy losses at fundamental frequency. The third-order high-pass filter presents greater operating losses than the second-order high-pass filter and is less effective for the filtering action. [13]

4.0 Conclusion

In this paper a dynamic model design and simulation of wind farm model, HVDC offshore and onshore model and HVDC model with LCC is presented. The design of a wind farm with eight generators, where three are FSC generator and four are DFIG wind turbines. The harmonic load flow is performed on all the model, there was zero harmonics since all the built-in equipment’s were functioning in ideal condition. The negative sequence harmonics are to be high since the rectifier used is 6-pulse. Therefore, harmonics currents were injected manually according to literature in DFIG and FCS generator of wind farm model, in converters in case of HVDC model. The total harmonic distortion (THD) voltage was analysed after plotting in bar graph. In case of wind farms single-tuned filters for harmonic order 5, 7 and 11 were designed for all individual wind turbines and two double-tuned filters at PCC, to analyse and compare which placement of filter will be the best. It is found that for effective mitigation of harmonics the filters are to be designed near the harmonic source i.e., at individual wind turbines and at PCC only to mitigate harmonics where the users will be connected. In case of HVDC transmission system onshore and offshore 3 single-tuned filters were designed at both onshore side and offshore side. In HVDC LCC model two double-tuned filters are designed on both rectifier and inverter side of the transmission link. The harmonics where mitigated successfully in all three models to meet the limits of IEEE 519-2014 standards.
